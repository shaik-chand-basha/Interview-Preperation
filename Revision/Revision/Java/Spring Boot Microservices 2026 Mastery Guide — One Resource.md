Two-Phase Commit (2PC)</h2><p id="13b9" class="pw-post-body-paragraph ni nj gv nk b hp nl nm nn hs no np nq nr ns nt nu nv nw nx ny nz oa ob oc od go bg" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong class="nk gw">Two-Phase Commit</strong> is a distributed transaction protocol used to ensure that all involved services either commit or roll back the transaction. In the first phase, all services involved in the transaction vote whether they can commit the transaction. If all services agree, the transaction is committed in the second phase. If any service disagrees, the transaction is rolled back.</p><p id="78b1" class="pw-post-body-paragraph ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od go bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">While 2PC can work for distributed transactions, it comes with some limitations:</p><ul class=""><li id="1069" class="ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od zg yn yo bg" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong class="nk gw">Performance Impact</strong>: It can lead to latency and performance bottlenecks, especially when there are many services involved.</li><li id="0a70" class="ni nj gv nk b hp yp nm nn hs yq np nq nr yr nt nu nv ys nx ny nz yt ob oc od zg yn yo bg" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong class="nk gw">Blocking Nature</strong>: It blocks the participating services until the transaction is finalized, which could cause issues in highly distributed systems.</li></ul><p id="1fa1" class="pw-post-body-paragraph ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od go bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">Because of these limitations, <strong class="nk gw">2PC</strong> is rarely used in microservices architectures.</p><h2 id="3e4e" class="mm mn gv bb mo mp mq hr mr ms mt hu mu mv mw mx my mz na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">2. Saga Pattern</h2><p id="07e6" class="pw-post-body-paragraph ni nj gv nk b hp nl nm nn hs no np nq nr ns nt nu nv nw nx ny nz oa ob oc od go bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">The <strong class="nk gw">Saga Pattern</strong> is a more scalable and flexible solution for managing distributed transactions in microservices. Rather than relying on a single, monolithic transaction, a saga splits the transaction into a series of smaller, isolated transactions, each handled by a different service. Each service is responsible for committing its local transaction.</p><p id="45b9" class="pw-post-body-paragraph ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od go bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">There are two types of sagas:</p><p id="fb5f" class="pw-post-body-paragraph ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od go bg" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong class="nk gw">Choreography-Based Saga</strong>:</p><ul class=""><li id="f50b" class="ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od zg yn yo bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">Services interact with each other by emitting events. Each service listens for events from other services and performs its part of the transaction. There is no central coordinator.</li><li id="d88d" class="ni nj gv nk b hp yp nm nn hs yq np nq nr yr nt nu nv ys nx ny nz yt ob oc od zg yn yo bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">Example: Service A processes an order and emits an event. Service B listens to the event and updates its records accordingly.</li></ul><p id="0b0b" class="pw-post-body-paragraph ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od go bg" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong class="nk gw">Orchestration-Based Saga</strong>:</p><ul class=""><li id="cd30" class="ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od zg yn yo bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">A central orchestrator or saga coordinator manages the transaction. It directs each service through the steps of the saga and handles compensating transactions if one of the steps fails.</li><li id="81bf" class="ni nj gv nk b hp yp nm nn hs yq np nq nr yr nt nu nv ys nx ny nz yt ob oc od zg yn yo bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">Example: A payment service might start the saga by requesting a payment, and an orchestrator coordinates the order processing, payment, and shipment steps.</li></ul><p id="a75a" class="pw-post-body-paragraph ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od go bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">The <strong class="nk gw">Saga Pattern</strong> offers significant advantages:</p><ul class=""><li id="aa5c" class="ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od zg yn yo bg" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong class="nk gw">Scalability</strong>: Each service is independent and only involved in a small part of the transaction.</li><li id="8926" class="ni nj gv nk b hp yp nm nn hs yq np nq nr yr nt nu nv ys nx ny nz yt ob oc od zg yn yo bg" data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong class="nk gw">Fault Tolerance</strong>: If one step in the saga fails, compensating transactions are triggered to undo the previous steps.</li></ul><p id="e0ce" class="pw-post-body-paragraph ni nj gv nk b hp oe nm nn hs of np nq nr og nt nu nv oh nx ny nz oi ob oc od go bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">This is the most commonly used pattern in microservices for handling distributed transactions.</p><h2 id="01de" class="mm mn gv bb mo mp mq hr mr ms mt hu mu mv mw mx my mz na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh bg" data-selectable-paragraph="">3.
